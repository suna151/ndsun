TWO DIMENSIONAL STUDY



This mixed media drawing and collage was created after the extensive research that Dahee and I conducted. I wanted to capture the emotions behind my dysphoria of being a first generation Asian American along with the alienation that comes from being a gay man. The Mandarin characters on the top of the piece translates to, “I like men” and the colors used for these characters are mimicked throughout the piece. The two figures, which are me, are inevitably intertwined, symbolized by the red thread and needle connecting the two figures. The figures, have pieces cut out, which references the brokenness I feel as an individual struggling with my identity. The red thread, specifically in Asian culture, is also known as the red thread of fate, which binds two people together. However, this red thread of fate binds me together to the other half of my self. These two halves are the embrace of my sexuality and my ethnicity, on the right, and my feelings of displacement and aloneness, on the left. These two halves of myself, although conflicting, will always be a part of me, and through creating this piece, I realized that these conflicting halves of myself are ultimately intertwined.

THREE DIMENSIONAL STUDY


This three dimensional structure was created after I synthesized more information from my two dimensional study. I tried to push myself through this sculpture and create something along the lines of the abstract fine art realm. This piece was created by putting wooden sticks together and using the pressure of the string that I hand wrapped around each stick to hold the triangle in place. After the triangle was created, I added more hanging string to the form, to make it look more worn and tattered. Finally, I attached black felt to the end of the triangle. This sculpture was roughly designed based off of Taiwanese paper lanterns. The pink triangle references the pink triangle symbol that was used in World War II to mark individuals who were gay. The black felt, which symbolizes my hair, is a reference to the asian notion that cutting your hair means severing your ties and starting anew. Through this piece, I am trying to sever my ties with my homosexuality and my Asian heritage, however the sculpture acts almost like a cage, keeping me imprisoned in the person I am.
